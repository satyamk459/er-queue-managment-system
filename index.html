The Critical Nature of ER Triage

In an Emergency Room, the traditional First-Come, First-Served (FCFS) model is not just inefficient—it's dangerous. A patient with cardiac arrest cannot be prioritized over someone experiencing a minor injury. Our system leverages Priority Queues to ensure that medical resources are directed where they are needed most, instantaneously.

Concept Details

Core Data Structures

Priority Queue (Min-Heap)

Used for primary triage ordering. Ensures O(log n) insertion and extraction of highest priority patients.

Insert O(log n) Extract O(log n)

Circular Queue

Manages patient inflow for entry-stage level. Handles efficient memory usage for fixed-size waiting areas.

Enqueue O(1) Dequeue O(1)

Doubly Linked List

The master log for all patient histories. Allows O(1) deletion and traversal in any direction without sequential search.

Append O(1) Delete O(1)

Hash Table

Instant lookup of patient data using their ID. Confirmed linear search times during critical lookups.

Search O(1) Avg Case O(1)

Stack

Implements the "undo" functionality for triage actions. Tracks the history of medical decisions.

Push O(1) Pop O(1)

The Architecture of Efficiency

By selecting a Min-Heap as the primary driver for our queue, we ensure that the triage process remains lightning fast, regardless of whether we have 10 or 1,000 patients. The time complexity of O(log n) means that even with a massive influx of patients (e.g., during a mass casualty event), the system will re-order priority almost instantaneously.

  • Memory-efficient circular buffer management.
  • Conflict-free hash-based patient identification.
